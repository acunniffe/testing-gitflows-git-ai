# The Lighthouse Keeper's Last Signal

The lighthouse had stood on the cliff for over a hundred years, but tonight would be its last night of service. Mara climbed the iron spiral staircase one final time, her boots ringing against each step like a bell tolling.

At the top, the great lens waited — a cathedral of glass and brass that had guided thousands of ships safely past the rocks below. Tomorrow, they would replace it with an automated LED array. No keeper required.

She lit the lamp the old way, with a match struck against the wall. The flame caught, and the lens began its slow rotation, throwing a blade of light across the dark water.

A storm was rolling in from the west. She could smell it — salt and ozone and something older. Through her binoculars, she spotted a small sailboat struggling against the swells, its mast tilting at dangerous angles.

Mara grabbed the radio. Static. She tried again. Nothing.

She looked at the lamp, then at the boat, then at the emergency signal panel — a relic from the 1940s that no one had used in decades. Three switches, each triggering a different pattern of light. She flipped the first one: *danger, alter course*.

The lens shifted its rhythm, pulsing in sharp bursts instead of its steady sweep. For a long moment, nothing happened. Then, slowly, the sailboat turned. It swung wide of the rocks, riding the edge of the storm into the calm waters of the bay.

Mara watched until the boat's anchor light held steady in the harbor. She sat down in the wooden chair that every keeper before her had worn smooth and let out a breath she didn't know she'd been holding.

In the morning, the technicians arrived with their equipment. They asked her if the last night had been quiet.

"Quiet enough," she said, and handed over the key.
