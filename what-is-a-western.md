# What Is a Western?

A **western** is a genre of fiction set primarily in the American Old West, typically between the late 18th century and the early 20th century. The genre spans film, literature, television, and other media.

## Key Characteristics

- **Setting**: The frontier landscapes of the American West — deserts, plains, small towns, ranches, and saloons.
- **Time period**: Most commonly the post-Civil War era through the early 1900s, during westward expansion.
- **Themes**: Lawlessness vs. justice, individualism, survival, civilization vs. wilderness, and moral ambiguity.
- **Common figures**: Cowboys, outlaws, sheriffs, Native Americans, settlers, and gunslingers.

## Hallmarks of the Genre

- Standoffs and gunfights
- Horse riding and cattle drives
- Conflicts over land, gold, or honor
- A lone protagonist navigating a harsh, lawless world

## Notable Examples

- **Film**: *The Good, the Bad and the Ugly* (1966), *Unforgiven* (1992), *True Grit* (2010)
- **Literature**: *Lonesome Dove* by Larry McMurtry, *Blood Meridian* by Cormac McCarthy
- **Television**: *Deadwood*, *Yellowstone*

## Subgenres

- **Spaghetti Western**: Italian-produced westerns, popularized by director Sergio Leone.
- **Revisionist Western**: Challenges traditional genre tropes, often presenting a more nuanced or critical view of the frontier era.
- **Neo-Western**: Set in the modern day but retaining western themes and aesthetics.
- **Space Western**: Blends western elements with science fiction (e.g., *Firefly*).
