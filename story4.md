# The Beekeeper Who Heard Colors

The first time it happened, Jonah thought the hive was singing.

He had been keeping bees for twenty years — long enough to know their moods by sound alone. The low hum of contentment. The sharp whine of agitation before a storm. But this was different. When the queen's attendants fanned their wings that morning, he didn't just hear the vibration. He saw it — a ribbon of deep amber unspooling through the air like smoke.

His doctor said it was synesthesia, probably triggered by the mild stroke he'd had in March. Nothing dangerous. His brain was simply cross-wiring sound and color. It would likely fade.

It didn't fade.

By summer, every hive had its own palette. The colony near the orchard hummed in shades of copper and rose — they were his calmest bees, the ones that never stung. The hive by the stone wall pulsed with violet, almost electric, and produced twice the honey of any other. The smallest colony, the one he'd nearly lost to mites in spring, flickered between grey and a pale, uncertain green.

He learned to read the colors the way he had once read the sounds. Grey meant disease. A flash of red meant the queen was failing. And gold — true gold, not amber or yellow but the color of light through old church windows — meant the hive was thriving in a way that went beyond mere survival.

One evening in August, he was sitting beside the orchard hive when all the colonies began to hum at once. The air filled with color — layers of it, braiding together like threads on a loom. For a moment, the entire apiary blazed with that impossible gold.

Then it passed. The bees settled. The colors dimmed to their usual murmur.

Jonah sat very still. He understood, in some way he couldn't have explained to his doctor or anyone else, that the bees had been talking to each other, and that he had been allowed to overhear.

He never told anyone what he saw. But he stopped wearing his veil after that. The bees never stung him again.
