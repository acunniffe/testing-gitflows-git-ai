# What Is an Eastern?

An **eastern** is a genre of fiction set in East Asia or the broader Asian continent, often drawing on the history, mythology, and martial traditions of the region. The genre spans film, literature, television, and other media.

## Key Characteristics

- **Setting**: Ancient temples, bamboo forests, imperial courts, bustling marketplaces, and remote mountain villages across East and Southeast Asia.
- **Time period**: Varies widely — from ancient dynastic eras to feudal Japan to modern-day settings with traditional roots.
- **Themes**: Honor, duty, revenge, spiritual discipline, the balance between chaos and order, and the pursuit of mastery.
- **Common figures**: Samurai, ronin, wuxia swordsmen, monks, emperors, ninjas, and wandering warriors.

## Hallmarks of the Genre

- Martial arts combat and sword fighting
- Quests for vengeance or redemption
- A master-student dynamic or a warrior's journey
- Conflicts rooted in clan loyalty, political intrigue, or spiritual trials

## Notable Examples

- **Film**: *Seven Samurai* (1954), *Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon* (2000), *Hero* (2002)
- **Literature**: *The Water Margin* by Shi Nai'an, *Musashi* by Eiji Yoshikawa
- **Television**: *Shogun*, *Kingdom*

## Subgenres

- **Wuxia**: Chinese martial arts fiction emphasizing chivalric heroes and fantastical combat.
- **Jidaigeki**: Japanese period dramas set in the Edo period or earlier, often featuring samurai.
- **Chambara**: Japanese sword-fighting films, the action-oriented counterpart to jidaigeki.
- **Martial Arts Film**: Broad category encompassing kung fu, karate, and other combat-focused stories across Asian cinema.
